Field experiment N° 005

The shape of return.

On reading as a non-linear act — and why a trail of dots is not enough.

A reader rarely travels through an essay in a straight line. They loop. They dwell on a paragraph, leap forward to confirm a suspicion, return to verify — and this looping is the essential motion of understanding. A list of stations cannot show it.

The problem with a line

In the previous experiment we drew a reading trail as a vertical line of stations, each spaced proportionally to time. The result was honest but flat: every paragraph visited appeared once in the list, and a paragraph visited three times looked like three separate events. The loop — the most meaningful gesture of deep reading — was invisible.

The question, then: what would a truthful representation of reading look like, if reading is not linear? We need a structure that shows not just what was read, but what was returned to. A graph, not a list.

One node per paragraph visited

Every paragraph that holds the reader's attention for three seconds or more becomes a node in the graph. If the reader visits the same paragraph again later, the existing node grows — in size, in color saturation, in the cumulative dwell time recorded against it.

This matters. A dense paragraph that rewards re-reading will appear as a heavy node, easy to spot. A transitional paragraph visited once will stay small. The topology of the essay, as experienced by this particular reader, is drawn in real typographic weight.

Forward and return arcs

When the reader moves from one paragraph to the next, an edge is drawn between the two nodes. Forward edges — to a paragraph not yet visited — are straight, thin, and gray. They are the expected motion.

But when the reader returns to a paragraph they've already seen, the edge is different. It curves right, in coral, as a Bézier arc. The geometry of return is encoded in the geometry of the edge. A graph with many curved coral arcs is a graph of a reader thinking; a graph of only straight gray lines is a graph of a reader merely passing through.

What the shape reveals

Look at the graph of a real session and three patterns emerge immediately. A dense knot of curves around two or three nodes means the reader wrestled with a passage. A long straight run of forward edges means the reader was convinced, or bored, and moved on. A cluster of pins in one region of the graph means the reader found value there and wants to return.

These patterns are not inferred — they are directly visible. A glance at the graph, and the reader understands their own reading. No labels, no numbers, no explanation needed. The topology tells the story.

Topology over time

There is a choice to make in drawing such a graph: what goes on the vertical axis? The options are three. Spatial — nodes placed by their position in the document. Force-directed — a physical simulation. Or temporal — nodes placed by the timestamp of their first visit.

The Memory Graph uses temporal. The Y axis is time — the minute at which each node was first reached. The result is that the graph grows downward as you read, mirroring the direction of your own scroll. A loop appears as a curve returning upward, back to a node higher on the Y axis. You see, literally, yourself moving against time to re-read.

The metrics that matter

With the graph comes a vocabulary the trail could not offer. Loops — the number of times the reader returned to a previously-visited node. Deepest — the node with the highest cumulative dwell, which is often the paragraph the reader most wrestled with. Intensity — the distribution of attention across time, rendered as a sparkline that shows where the minutes went.

These are not vanity metrics. They are diagnostic. A reader who sees Loops: 0 on an essay they thought they understood deeply has just learned that they did not, in fact, return to verify anything — they read it like a novel.

What it does not do

It does not send your reading pattern to a server. It does not sync across devices. It does not feed recommendation algorithms. The graph lives in localStorage and in the reader's own head. It is a mirror, not a lens.

And it does not replace the essay. The graph is a second surface — invoked on demand, closed when done. It reports on the reading; it is not itself a document to be read. When the reader closes the panel, the article resumes, exactly as it was.

A signature for the reader

Two readers who read the same essay produce two different graphs. One may be a straight descending path with no returns. Another may be a dense thicket of loops around three central nodes. Neither is better — but they are different readings, and the graphs make the difference visible.

If you export your graph as JSON and share it with a friend who has read the same essay, you can compare. Here is what I found heavy, what did you? The graph is not just a tool for individual memory — it is the beginning of a conversation about reading itself.

A last thought

The Memory Graph is not a performance metric. It is not analytics. It is a small, local, personal drawing — the shape of your attention, on this essay, at this moment.

Close the panel. Scroll back if you want. Your graph will be waiting, unchanged, the next time you need to see where you have been.

Reading the graph

Six visual grammars.

Size is dwell.

A node's radius grows with total time spent on that paragraph. A paragraph read briefly is a small dot; a paragraph returned to and lingered on is a heavy node.

Forward edges are straight.

When you move to a paragraph you have not visited yet, a thin gray line connects them. These are the expected motions of reading.

Return edges are curved.

When you go back to a paragraph already visited, the edge curves right in coral. The geometry of return is encoded in the geometry of the arc.

Rings mean pinned.

A coral ring orbiting a node marks a paragraph you chose to pin with P. Pins survive across sessions.

Pulse is current.

The node that is currently in your attention band pulses softly. It's the one paragraph the graph considers live.

Gray dots are passages.

When the Show passages toggle is on, paragraphs you only scrolled past appear as tiny gray dots. The graph becomes more honest but more crowded.

Pure SVG, pure localStorage, pure JavaScript. No D3, no force simulation, no graph library. The layout is deterministic: one pass over events, one pass over unique nodes. 
/* THE DATA MODEL */
const nodes = new Map();  // paraId → { order, firstAt, totalMs, visits, pinned, extract }
const edges = [];          // [{ from, to, kind: 'forward' | 'return', at }]
let previousParaId = null;

/* CAPTURING A VISIT */
function visit(paraId, dwellMs) {
  if (dwellMs < DWELL_MS) return;

  // Record the transition as an edge (if we were somewhere before)
  if (previousParaId && previousParaId !== paraId) {
    const kind = nodes.has(paraId) ? 'return' : 'forward';
    edges.push({ from: previousParaId, to: paraId, kind, at: Date.now() });
  }

  // Create or update the node
  const existing = nodes.get(paraId);
  if (existing) {
    existing.totalMs += dwellMs;
    existing.visits += 1;
  } else {
    nodes.set(paraId, {
      order: nodes.size,
      firstAt: Date.now(),
      totalMs: dwellMs,
      visits: 1,
      pinned: false,
      extract: extractFirstWords(paraId, 80),
    });
  }

  previousParaId = paraId;
}

/* RENDERING — one SVG, one pass */
function renderGraph() {
  const sorted = [...nodes.entries()].sort((a, b) => a[1].firstAt - b[1].firstAt);

  // Y position from firstAt, X position alternates to prevent overlap
  const positions = new Map();
  sorted.forEach(([id, data], i) => {
    const y = mapTimeToY(data.firstAt);
    const x = CENTER_X + ((i % 2 === 0) ? -NODE_OFFSET : NODE_OFFSET);
    positions.set(id, { x, y });
  });

  // Draw edges first (under the nodes)
  edges.forEach(e => {
    const p1 = positions.get(e.from);
    const p2 = positions.get(e.to);
    if (!p1 || !p2) return;
    if (e.kind === 'return') {
      // Bézier curve bending right
      const midY = (p1.y + p2.y) / 2;
      const ctrl = { x: CENTER_X + RETURN_BEND, y: midY };
      drawPath(`M ${p1.x} ${p1.y} Q ${ctrl.x} ${ctrl.y} ${p2.x} ${p2.y}`, 'return-edge');
    } else {
      drawLine(p1, p2, 'forward-edge');
    }
  });

  // Draw nodes
  sorted.forEach(([id, data]) => {
    const pos = positions.get(id);
    const radius = nodeRadiusFor(data);
    drawCircle(pos, radius, data);
  });
}
— five ways to break it —

A graph that lies is worse than no graph at all.

01 · DO NOT
Use force simulation.
A force-directed layout reshuffles the graph every time the reader opens it. Position becomes meaningless — you can't point to a node and say "that's the heavy one up there" if "up there" changes. The temporal axis is not negotiable.
02 · DO NOT
Draw every scroll event.
A graph that includes every paragraph the cursor briefly crossed becomes a scribble. The three-second dwell floor is the tariff of signal-over-noise. Reading, not scrolling, creates nodes.
03 · DO NOT
Animate nodes on every update.
The graph is a still life, not a live chart. Animation is reserved for invocation (slide-in of the panel) and liveness (the pulse on the current node). A graph that wiggles while you read it is a graph that cannot be read.
04 · DO NOT
Color nodes by topic.
Tempting, but wrong. Coloring nodes by the topic of their paragraph asks the component to classify content — a job it has no business doing. One color for pinned, one for current, one for everything else. Restraint is the feature.
05 · DO NOT
Send the graph anywhere.
The graph is a portrait of the reader's thinking. It stays on the reader's device. Exporting to JSON is explicit and local; anything else — analytics, recommendation seeds, user profiles — turns the component into its opposite.
